Website — Momint

Redesigning the Momint marketing website to clearly communicate the platform's value proposition to two distinct audiences — creators and collectors — and improve the quality of user acquisition.

Role Product Manager
Company Momint
Year 2022–2023
Platform Web
Overview

The Momint website was built during the initial product launch and had not been substantially updated since. As the product matured and the positioning became clearer, the website no longer accurately represented what Momint was or who it was for.

This project covers the redesign of the Momint marketing site — from positioning and messaging strategy through to design and delivery — with a focus on improving the quality of organic and paid traffic acquisition and better converting visitors into registered users.

My Role

I led the product and messaging strategy for the website redesign, working with a product designer on the visual treatment and with the CEO on positioning decisions. I owned the sitemap, the copy brief, and the conversion goals for each page.

This was one of the projects where my background in product design was most directly useful — I was able to move quickly between strategic positioning questions and concrete design decisions without needing a long brief-to-brief handoff.

Background

The original Momint website leaned heavily on NFT-native language and imagery that resonated with early adopters but actively alienated mainstream users. As the product strategy shifted toward a broader collector audience — people interested in digital art, not blockchain technology — the website had become a mismatch with the acquisition goals.

Data from paid campaigns confirmed the problem: landing page conversion rates were low, and the quality of users who did sign up from the site was inconsistent. A clearer, more targeted site was expected to improve both metrics.

[ Image placeholder ]
Understanding the Problem

The core problem was messaging clarity. The existing site could not answer the question "what is Momint and why should I care?" for a visitor who did not already know what an NFT marketplace was. The jargon was a barrier, and the visual presentation prioritised aesthetic over communication.

A second problem was audience targeting: the site was trying to speak to creators and collectors simultaneously, without clearly separating the two value propositions. Creators needed to understand what the platform could do for them as publishers; collectors needed to understand what they could discover and own. A single undifferentiated message served neither well.

Understanding the User

We ran a targeted research exercise focused on first impressions — recruiting six people who had not previously heard of Momint and observing their reactions to the existing site. The exercise was structured around specific questions: what does this product do? Who is it for? Would you sign up?

The results were blunt. None of the participants could confidently describe what Momint was after viewing the homepage. Most assumed it was a crypto trading platform rather than a marketplace for digital art. The disconnect between the product reality and the website representation was larger than we had expected.

[ Image placeholder ]
Affinity Mapping

Research findings were synthesised into themes covering: comprehension failures, language barriers, trust signals that resonated, visual elements that helped versus confused, and the specific questions visitors had that the existing site did not answer.

The comprehension failures cluster was the most actionable. It produced a clear list of the things the new site had to communicate within the first scroll: what you can buy, who makes it, how much it costs, and why Momint is a trustworthy place to spend money. Everything else was secondary.

Product Vision & Solution

The vision for the redesigned site was a clean, confident presentation that led with art — not technology. The homepage would open on beautiful work from Momint creators, with a simple, jargon-free explanation of what the platform was and who it was for.

The solution introduced separate journeys for creators and collectors from the homepage, with distinct landing pages for each audience. The creator page focused on reach, earnings, and the simplicity of listing work. The collector page focused on discovery, provenance, and the quality of the creator community.

All blockchain and NFT terminology was reviewed and either replaced with plain language equivalents or removed entirely where it added no useful information for the target audience.

Wireframes & Design Iterations

The wireframe process began with the homepage, which required the most iteration. We explored three distinct structural approaches before settling on one that led with a featured artwork hero, followed by a clear dual-audience navigation into the creator and collector journeys.

Copy was written in parallel with design — unusual for this team, but necessary because the messaging decisions were as important as the layout decisions. The copy brief was reviewed by the CEO before any visual design was applied to ensure the positioning was aligned at the highest level before committing to execution.

Developer Handover

The website was built by a front-end developer who was not part of the core product team, which made handover documentation especially important. I produced a page-by-page specification covering layout, copy, interactive elements, and performance requirements — including image optimisation expectations and target load times.

A content management requirement was also specified: the team needed to be able to update featured creators and artworks on the homepage without a developer involved. This shaped the technical architecture of the homepage hero section.

[ Image placeholder ]
Production Prototype

The redesigned site launched and was used as the primary landing destination for paid acquisition campaigns. Early signals on landing page conversion were positive, and the qualitative feedback from the partnerships team indicated that the creator-focused page was significantly improving initial conversations with prospective creators.

A repeat of the first-impression research exercise post-launch confirmed that the comprehension failures identified in the original research had been resolved — participants could now accurately describe what Momint was and who it was for within thirty seconds of landing on the homepage.

Vibe Coding — IOnlyListenToVinyl

Designing and building a personal web app for vinyl record discovery and collection management — conceived, specced, and shipped solo using an AI-native workflow.

Role Product Manager / Builder
Company Personal Project
Year 2024
Platform Web App
Overview

IOnlyListenToVinyl is a personal web app built to scratch a genuine itch: a better way to discover new vinyl records based on what I already love, and to track what I own, want, and have listened to. The project was also an experiment in solo AI-native product development — from initial concept through to a working prototype.

This case study covers the thinking behind the product, the process used to define and build it, and what I learned about shipping as a solo PM-builder using AI tooling throughout.

My Role

This was a solo project. I owned every part of the process: product definition, user research (self and adjacent), information architecture, visual design direction, and technical scoping. I used AI tooling to accelerate the build, acting as the decision-maker and quality bar throughout.

The project was deliberately structured as a PM would run a team sprint — with a written spec, defined acceptance criteria, and a deliberate build order — to test whether that discipline holds when applied to personal work.

Background

I have been collecting vinyl for several years and found the existing tools — Discogs, Rate Your Music — useful for cataloguing but poor at helping me discover what to buy next based on my actual taste. Most recommendation engines optimise for popularity, not personal preference depth.

The idea for IOnlyListenToVinyl came from a simple frustration: I wanted to type in a record I loved and get back five records I would almost certainly also love — not a list of the bestsellers in the same genre.

Understanding the Problem

The core problem was discovery friction: the gap between hearing something you love and finding the next thing you will love is larger than it should be, especially in the vinyl-specific context where streaming services cannot tell you what is actually available to buy.

A secondary problem was collection sprawl: want lists scattered across apps, purchase history in emails, and listening notes in Notes app. There was no single place that held the full picture of what I owned, what I wanted, and what I had listened to recently.

I framed the product as a tool that solved both: a smart discovery layer on top of a lightweight personal collection manager.

Understanding the User

I was the primary user, which made the initial research efficient but required deliberate effort to avoid building purely for myself. I interviewed four other regular vinyl buyers to pressure-test whether the problems I was solving were shared.

The conversations confirmed the discovery problem was widely felt. The collection management problem was more varied — some people had robust systems already; others were as disorganised as I was. This shaped the decision to make discovery the primary value proposition and collection management a lighter supporting feature.

[ Image placeholder ]
Affinity Mapping

After the interviews I mapped findings into a simple affinity diagram covering: discovery pain points, collection management habits, trust signals when buying, and what made a recommendation feel credible versus generic.

The key insight from this exercise was that credibility in music recommendation comes from specificity — people trusted recommendations that referenced concrete shared references, not broad genre labels. This informed how the app surfaces its suggestions.

Product Vision & Solution

The product vision was a tool that felt like a knowledgeable friend who knows your collection — one who can say "if you love that, you need to hear this" with real specificity. Minimal UI, fast interactions, no algorithm opacity.

The v1 solution focused on three screens: a search and discovery view where you enter a record you love and receive curated recommendations with context; a collection view where you log owned records and tag listening status; and a want list that persists across sessions.

The technical approach used the Discogs API for catalogue data and a recommendation layer built on top of genre, label, year, and personnel metadata — with the ability to weight results toward less well-known records.

Wireframes & Design Iterations

Design went through two main rounds. The first was a rough wireframe pass focused entirely on the discovery flow — how a user enters a reference record, what the results page shows, and how much context to surface per recommendation. I tested this with the same four interview participants.

The second round refined the visual treatment, tightened the collection management interaction model, and resolved several edge cases around records with no Discogs match. The final design was intentionally spare — the music should be the focus, not the interface.

Developer Handover

As a solo build, "developer handover" meant writing a spec detailed enough that I could hand it to an AI coding assistant and get back working code with minimal back-and-forth. This required the same rigour as a real handover — clear acceptance criteria, annotated designs, and explicit edge case handling.

The discipline of writing a proper spec before building proved its value: the AI generated fewer wrong assumptions when given precise context. Sections where I was vague in the spec required the most iteration to get right.

[ Image placeholder ]
Production Prototype

The working prototype was completed and deployed in 2024. The core discovery flow works end to end: enter a record, receive recommendations with rationale, save to want list. Collection management is functional at a basic level.

The project confirmed the value of applying PM discipline to personal builds. The spec-first approach produced a better result faster than previous personal projects where I had started building without a clear plan. It also produced a compelling demonstration of what an AI-native PM workflow looks like in practice.

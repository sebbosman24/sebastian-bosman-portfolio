Client Dashboard — Momint

Building a self-serve analytics and management dashboard for creators and brand partners on the Momint platform — reducing support overhead and increasing creator retention.

Role Product Manager
Company Momint
Year 2022–2023
Platform Web App
Overview

Momint's creator and brand partners had no dedicated tooling for managing their presence on the platform. Sales data, drop performance, and collector information was either not exposed to creators at all, or required a manual request to the Momint team to retrieve.

This project covers the design and delivery of a self-serve dashboard that gave creators visibility into their performance, control over their profile and upcoming drops, and access to the data they needed to make informed decisions about how they used the platform.

My Role

I led the product work end to end — from stakeholder discovery with the partnerships team through to delivery. I worked with a product designer on the UI and with the engineering lead to define what was achievable within the data infrastructure already in place.

I owned the prioritisation of dashboard features, the definition of the metrics to surface, and the decisions around what level of data access to grant to different creator tiers. I also ran the QA process before launch and collected post-launch feedback for the next iteration.

Background

The absence of creator-facing tooling had become a meaningful retention risk. More sophisticated creators — those with existing audiences and commercial ambitions — were beginning to ask pointed questions about performance data and were comparing Momint unfavourably to other platforms that offered self-serve analytics.

The internal support team was also spending a disproportionate amount of time fielding data requests that could have been handled by the creators themselves if the right tooling existed. The business case for the dashboard was clear on two dimensions: retention and operational efficiency.

[ Image placeholder ]
Understanding the Problem

The core problem was information asymmetry: Momint had data that creators needed, but no mechanism to deliver it. This created a dependency on the support team for every data-related question, and left creators feeling underserved and out of control of their own presence on the platform.

A secondary problem was the range of sophistication across the creator base. Some creators were data-literate and wanted granular metrics; others simply wanted to know how their last drop performed and when they would be paid. The dashboard needed to serve both without overwhelming either.

Understanding the User

We conducted structured interviews with eight creators across the full range of platform engagement — from occasional sellers to high-volume partners with regular drop schedules. We also interviewed two members of the partnerships team to understand the patterns in creator support requests.

The interviews produced a clear hierarchy of creator needs: earnings visibility first, drop performance second, collector data third, and profile management fourth. This hierarchy directly shaped the information architecture of the dashboard.

[ Image placeholder ]
Affinity Mapping

We synthesised interview findings with the partnerships team using an affinity mapping session. Clusters emerged around: earning transparency, drop lifecycle visibility, collector relationship management, platform trust, and the comparative experience of other creator platforms the interviewees had used.

The comparative cluster was particularly useful — creators named specific features from other platforms they found valuable, which gave us a concrete target to match on the highest-priority items without over-specifying the full feature set upfront.

Product Vision & Solution

The vision was a dashboard that made creators feel like professional partners rather than platform guests — with full visibility into their earnings, drops, and collector base, and the ability to manage their profile without relying on the Momint team.

The v1 solution comprised four core views: an earnings summary with payout history and pending amounts; a drop performance view showing sales, views, and conversion by drop; a collector overview listing who owned their work; and a profile editor for bio, avatar, and social links.

We deliberately scoped v1 narrowly to ship within the available sprint budget, with a clear backlog of v2 features covering more granular analytics, CSV export, and scheduled drop management.

Wireframes & Design Iterations

We moved quickly to low-fidelity wireframes given the well-defined user needs from research. The first round of wireframes covered all four views and was validated with three creators in a moderated session. Feedback focused primarily on the earnings view — creators wanted more granularity than the initial design provided.

The second round addressed the earnings feedback, refined the drop performance charts, and resolved several navigation and state management questions raised during the engineering review. A third pass addressed final QA findings before handover.

Developer Handover

Handover involved a detailed PRD covering data requirements, permission model, and all edge cases — including empty states for creators with no drops, creators with pending payouts, and first-time users who had not yet set up their profile.

The data layer was the most complex part of the handover. I worked closely with the engineering lead to define exactly which data points were available from existing infrastructure and which would require new instrumentation — and adjusted the v1 scope accordingly to avoid blocking the launch on data work that could follow.

[ Image placeholder ]
Production Prototype

The dashboard shipped to a closed beta of ten creators before a broader rollout. Beta feedback was positive — the earnings view in particular received strong responses, with creators describing it as "long overdue." Support ticket volume for data-related requests dropped noticeably in the weeks following the wider launch.

The dashboard became a meaningful part of the Momint creator pitch, used by the partnerships team as evidence of the platform's commitment to creator success. Plans for v2 analytics features were in development at the time this case study was written.
